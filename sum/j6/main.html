Reflections on Classroom Observation

This week at Loften High School, I arrived during a testing period, which prevented me from directly observing student engagement in the classroom. Instead, I collaborated with Eric on developing a teaching plan for the upcoming week. During our planning session, Eric shared a significant shift he has observed in student motivation over his teaching career. He explained that when he first began teaching, approximately 80% of students would complete assignments when he posted them and explained the reasoning behind the work. Now, however, he finds that simply providing rationale is insufficient. He must instead discover how the assignment's purpose aligns with each student's personal motivations and individual "why." This observation sparked an important discussion about the evolving nature of student engagement and the need to create more personalized connections between curriculum and student interests.

Moving forward, I intend to explore strategies for helping Eric bridge this motivation gap, perhaps by incorporating student choice, real-world applications, or more explicit connections between assignments and students' future goals or current interests. Understanding what drives today's students requires a more nuanced approach than in previous generations, and I recognize that effective teaching now demands greater flexibility in meeting students where they are.

Comfortability: 8/10 I chose this rating because while I felt comfortable collaborating with Eric on lesson planning, I recognize that not observing actual student interactions limited my ability to fully grasp the motivation challenges he described and develop concrete, classroom-tested solutions.
